If you are having trouble using this application, or would like to learn more about cryptocurrencies, please read the information below:

  1. What are cryptocurrencies

    Cryptocurrencies are decentralized virtual money units that are recorded on data files called blockchains. Cryptocurrencies are decentralized in that they have no single controlling entity, such as a government or company. Rather, cryptocurrencies represent unique keys that have their entire transaction history stored on decentralized blocks. These blocks, which together make up the blockchain, are stored by no single user and record all previous and subsequent transaction histories (Berentsen and Schär 2018). Additional virtual money units can be created in an act known as “mining” by solving algorithms that add new blocks to the blockchain. While there is no limit to the number of blocks that can be added to the blockchain, some currencies, including bitcoin, have limits to the number of coins that are earned in the mining process (Berentsen and Schär 2018).

    The first cryptocurrency, Bitcoin, was proposed in a 2008 white paper, written under the pseudonym “Satoshi Nakamoto,” called “Bitcoin – A Peer to Peer Electronic Cash System”. The software behind Bitcoin was made public in 2009 and it began trading in 2010. Bitcoin was followed by hundreds, if not thousands, of other cryptocurrencies, some of which were also designed specifically to be monetary units and others that inadvertently became monetary units (Marr 2017). At the time of this paper, Bitcoin has the highest market cap of any cryptocurrency, at 158.4 billion USD (Cryptocurrency Market Capitalizations n.d.). Ethereum, an originally non-monetary coin created in 2015 (Hertig n.d.), is second at 67.5 billion USD (Cryptocurrency Market Capitalizations n.d.). Litecoin, created in 2011 as one of the first successors to Bitcoin (Marr 2017), is seventh in market cap at 8.5 billion USD (Cryptocurrency Market Capitalizations n.d.)

  2. How are cryptocurrencies traded?

    Cryptocurrencies are generally traded on exchanges, of which dozens exist world wide, that allow users to sell and buy coins for issued currencies, like USD or EUR, or exchange them for other cryptocurrencies. Prices on these exchanges are determined by the aggregated rates at which sellers and buyers reach agreements and complete transactions (Berentsen and Schär 2018). Sellers list coins for given prices and transition ownership to buyers when buyers agree to buy for a price more or equal to the listed sale price. The list price of cryptocurrencies on a given exchange is the average of recent sale prices on that exchange. Similarly, the aggregated list price of cryptocurrencies across exchanges is calculated by volume weighted average price between exchanges (Madeira 2018).

    Because aggregated coin prices require weighted volumes for all exchanges, and because there is no single government or corporate entity to determine the exchanges that should be included in the aggregated price, final prices can be considered subjective since aggregators can choose to include or not include particular exchanges as they see fit. Cryptocompare.com, the price source used for this study, acknowledges that it excludes some exchanges due to, but not limited to, small volumes and trading irregularities (Madeira 2018).
